Case Study:
Redesigning the FCC website to help its users
access information easily and efficiently.

Background

The Federal Communications Commission regulates interstate and international communications by radio, television, wire, satellite, and cable in all 50 states, the District of Columbia and U.S. territories. The Commission is the federal agency responsible for implementing and enforcing America’s communications law and regulations.


The Challenge

The current website has significant redundancies, usability, accessibility and quality issues.

Access to stakeholders and users who have actually visited the website was limited. With that in mind, we identified problems a user would experience while trying to file a complaint.

The goal was to suggest solutions that would lead to efficiency in filing a complaint and to improve the overall experience and engagement on the website.

Solutions

To instill trust in individuals and respect with businesses, the FCC website needed to be updated to look energetic and modern. We did this by organizing content clearly, attaching a visual voice that is consistent and clear, to help citizens access information easily and without confusion.

  1. Connect with target audience via clear, descriptive call-to-action

  2. Consolidate navigation to remove redundencies

  3. Improve efficiency by consolidatiing content, providing clear prompts, and simplified user paths

  4. Improve UI for accessibility and to reduce cognitive overload

Proto Persona

John Blanchard, 47

John is a multi-tasking consumer policy lawyer, a dedicated volunteer for safe public housing and a protective father of two teenage girls. He prefers to have things work efficiently and purposefully so when he is not working he can spend his free time to outdoors and at his lake house with his wife and children.


Heuristic and
Color Analysis



Before conducting user tests for specific tasks, we decided to do an initial assessment of the interface. This helped us identify some basic usability issues that might impede process decisions.


  1. Layers of menu items without visual clarity made navigation confusing
  2. News section is taking valuable homepage space
  3. Colors failed the accessibility tests
  4. Low contrast between type and background
  5. Lack of structure and organization
  6. Long pages with heavy cognitive load

User Testing:
Current site

We created a User Testing Plan and conducted one-on-one interviews to evaluate the difficulties a user experiences while trying to complete the specified tasks.


Research Analysis

We used our user test findings to distill and categorize responses to discover pain points. We prioritized tasks that would inform the site structure and design.


Findings:

  1. Inconsistent pages across website
  2. Complicated navigation
  3. Too many paths with different titles for the same function
  4. Instructions were too long
  5. Not enough visual relief on Home page
  6. Links embedded into long copy, no clear CTA

Information
Architecture



A new information framework was created to simplify, strategize, plan and prioritize information within the website.


  1. Key menu items were renamed for targeted accessibility
  2. Expanded menu was designed for adding relevant, key information
  3. Pages were reorganized for easy access
  4. Menu items were consolidated logically
  5. Global navigation added for inclusivity and search capability

Brand Voice

The brand voice was identified and used as a reference for all decision making on the website design including language, hierarchy, visual voice and tone.
